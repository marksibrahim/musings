Real Numbers

What are the real numbers? They turn out to be uniquely determined by a handful of natural requirements on a set:
  • field (operations: +, x with identity, inverses, commutiativity, associativity, and distribution)
  • order: trichotomy
  • completeness (or least upper bound property)

careful: $\mathcal{R}$ doesn't include $\pm \infty$.
After defining the reals, we notice not all numbers are rational!

In fact, we see that $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \dots \sqrt{n}$ for any $n$ that's not a perfect square are all irrational

idea: use divisibility to show no rational number squared is 2

Cauchy Schwarz $\vert \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} \vert \leq \vert \vec{u} \vert \vert \vec{v} \vert$ Finally, Cantor showed $\mathcal{R}$ is uncountable!

idea: suppose someone claimed to have a list of all reals (this list would have to be infinite). Then, we write down a number that's different from each one on the list in the nth decimal place!

Topology

Abstraction is a powerful tool to show why Calculus works. We'll be able to prove key results in a more straightforward and generalizable form.


The key object of study is a metric space: a set M along with a distance function $d: MxM \rightarrow [0, \infty]$

  • $d$ is symmetric: $d(x, y) = d(y, x)$
  • definite $d(x, y) = 0 \iff x = y$
  • triangle: $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y,z)$

A neighborhood (or open ball) is a set $$B(a, r) = \left\{ x \vert\ d(a, x) < r \right\} $$ Another notion used to characterize closed sets is limit point (or accumulation point). We say $x$ is a limit point of a set $A$ if every $B(x, \epsilon)$ contains a point of $A$.
It turns out a set is closed $\iff$ the set contains all limit points. Closure is simply the set $\cup$ its limit points.

Compactness

Compactness is equivalent to sequential compactness, which means every sequence has a convergent subsequence.

Compact also implies complete

idea: sequential compactness and the sheep lemma (convergent sub $\implies$ whole seq converges)

Specialness in $\mathcal{R}$

Bolzano-Weirstrauss every bounded sequence in $\mathcal{R}^n$ has a convergent subsequence.

idea: look at bounding interval [-M, M] left or right half contains $\infty$-many terms; repeat and get convergent sub.

Heine-Borel In $\mathcal{R}n$ is compact is equivalent to closed and bounded.

trickier, but compact always implies closed and bounded no matter the metric space.

Compactness + Continuity

Now the real power of abstractness shines.
Before diving in, remember continuity also means $f^{-1}(open)$ is open. Open means any of the following:
  • there's a neighborhood around any point, albeit small, entirely contained in the set
  • in terms of sequences it means $f(x_n) \rightarrow f(p)$ if $x_n \rightarrow p$.

Continuous Image of Compact is dun dun dun...Compact

idea: look at $f^{-1}(open cover)$ get subcover for domain et voila

A nice consequence is that the $f(K)$ is actually uniformly continous!

The grand chain of amazing theorems:
Extreme Value Theorem $\implies$ Rolle's $\implies$ Mean Value Theorem

EVT: image is compact, get a sup b/c bounded, then get a convergent sub.
Rolle's: either both $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ are max and min
MVT: clever trick: use $g(x) = f(x) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a} (x-a)$ and Rolle's to get $g'(c) = 0$.
